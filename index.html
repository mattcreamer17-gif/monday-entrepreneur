The pattern

MondayEntrepreneur runs a weekly group, 1:1 sessions, a podcast, webinars, and four social channels, all out of a single founder. The marketing and content work around the community is almost certainly where your time is leaking, and the coaching itself is not. We watched the structure, listened to a few AbundanceNow episodes, and read through the four-pillar framing on the program page. The pattern is familiar, and it is solvable.

How we look at it

Most of what you do in a week is not an AI problem. It is structure. We look at small operators through a simple lens: roughly 60% of the work is traditional systems and databases, 30% is rule-based logic (templates, schedules, handoffs), and about 10% is genuinely a language-model problem (drafting, summarizing, translating).

Applied to MondayEntrepreneur, that probably means your member CRM and the Gallant handoff are system work, your content calendar and repurposing pipeline are rule-based, and a thin language-model layer sits on top to turn a 60-minute podcast into the week’s shorts, emails, and LinkedIn posts, with a Spanish pass if you want one.

The mistake most people make is putting an LLM where a spreadsheet would be faster, cheaper, and easier to audit. We help you draw that line clearly.

What the work looks like

We ran a scoped sprint with Feeld, a product company with a CTO trying to operationalize AI internally. A workshop, advisory calls, and an operational playbook over four weeks. At the end he had a written context architecture his team could actually use, and a clear view of where a model added real leverage against where it just added risk. The pattern scales down. A founder-led operation gets the same treatment, sized to a founder-led budget.

One relevant read

Interpretable Context Methodology (ICM). Published through ACM TiiS. It argues that your agent context should be organized as layered files any tool can read and any person can audit. Practical, not abstract. Repo: github.com/RinDig/Interpretable-Context-Methodology-ICM-.

Jake also built an online community to 22,000 members in five weeks. You may find that useful to compare notes on.
